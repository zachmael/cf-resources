## Why It Matters

We've spent the better part of three decades talking about climate change as a future threat. It's not future anymore. The impacts are here, and they're accelerating.

In 2023, the global average temperature exceeded 1.4°C above pre-industrial levels. The consequences are cascading through every sector: unprecedented wildfire seasons disrupting supply chains and destroying property, extreme rainfall events flooding manufacturing facilities and transportation networks, heat waves reducing labor productivity and stressing electrical grids, droughts threatening water-dependent industries from agriculture to semiconductor manufacturing.

Climate adaptation is the practice of preparing for these realities. While climate mitigation — reducing emissions — remains essential for limiting the severity of future impacts, adaptation addresses what's already happening and what's locked in by the greenhouse gases we've already emitted. Even in the most optimistic emissions scenarios, significant additional warming and its consequences are unavoidable over the coming decades.

For businesses, the stakes are concrete. Munich Re estimates that global insured losses from natural disasters reached $100 billion in 2023, and total economic losses were roughly triple that. Supply chain disruptions from extreme weather events have become a recurring feature of corporate earnings calls. Companies that haven't assessed their physical climate risk exposure — and developed plans to manage it — are operating with a significant blind spot.

## How It Works

### Understanding Physical Climate Risk

Climate adaptation starts with understanding your exposure. Physical climate risks fall into two categories:

**Acute risks** are event-driven: hurricanes, floods, wildfires, heat waves, and other extreme weather events. These are increasing in frequency and intensity. The probability distributions that companies and insurers have historically relied on are shifting, making historical experience an increasingly unreliable guide to future risk.

**Chronic risks** are longer-term shifts: rising sea levels, changing precipitation patterns, increasing average temperatures, ocean acidification, and shifting growing seasons. These unfold gradually but can fundamentally alter the viability of business operations, supply chains, and markets.

Both types of risk interact with your specific assets, operations, supply chains, and markets in ways that are highly location-dependent. A data center in Phoenix faces different climate risks than one in Amsterdam. A supply chain sourced from Southeast Asia faces different vulnerabilities than one sourced from Northern Europe.

### The Adaptation Planning Process

**Step 1: Climate risk screening.** Identify which climate hazards are most relevant to your operations and value chain, using climate projection data for the geographies where you operate and source materials. Tools like the IPCC climate atlas, national climate assessments, and commercial climate risk platforms provide location-specific projections under different warming scenarios.

**Step 2: Vulnerability assessment.** Not all exposure translates to vulnerability. A facility in a flood zone that's been elevated and hardened is less vulnerable than one that hasn't. Assess the sensitivity of your assets, operations, workforce, and supply chain to the identified hazards, considering existing protective measures and adaptive capacity.

**Step 3: Risk prioritization.** Map the combination of exposure, vulnerability, and potential consequences to prioritize where adaptation investment will have the greatest risk reduction impact. Consider both direct operational impacts and indirect effects through supply chains, markets, and community disruption.

**Step 4: Adaptation strategy development.** Design specific adaptation measures for your highest-priority risks:

- **Infrastructure and engineering solutions** — flood barriers, cooling systems, fire-resistant materials, elevated facilities, reinforced structures
- **Operational adjustments** — modified work schedules for extreme heat, alternative water sources, backup power systems, adjusted maintenance cycles
- **Supply chain diversification** — geographic diversification of sourcing, increased inventory buffers, alternative logistics routes, supplier resilience assessments
- **Financial instruments** — insurance optimization, catastrophe bonds, dedicated adaptation capital reserves
- **Nature-based solutions** — green infrastructure, watershed protection, mangrove restoration, urban tree canopy

**Step 5: Implementation and monitoring.** Execute adaptation measures with clear timelines, budgets, and accountability. Establish monitoring systems to track climate conditions, measure the effectiveness of adaptation actions, and trigger plan updates as conditions change or new information becomes available.

### Key Components of Effective Adaptation

**Scenario analysis** is foundational. Climate adaptation planning must consider multiple warming scenarios — typically 1.5°C, 2°C, and 3°C+ pathways — because the required adaptation measures differ significantly depending on the severity of warming. A facility might be adequately protected under 1.5°C of warming but critically exposed under 3°C.

**Time horizons matter.** Some adaptation measures have immediate payback (upgrading drainage, installing backup generators). Others require longer planning horizons (relocating facilities, redesigning products for different climate conditions). Capital allocation decisions need to account for the trajectory of risk, not just today's conditions.

**Community context is essential.** Businesses don't operate in isolation. Your facility's resilience depends partly on the resilience of surrounding infrastructure — transportation networks, power grids, water systems, emergency services. Engaging with local and regional adaptation planning processes is both good strategy and good citizenship.

## Council Fire's Perspective

Climate adaptation is where we see the biggest gap between awareness and action. Most corporate leaders understand that physical climate risks are increasing. Far fewer have translated that understanding into concrete risk assessments, prioritized adaptation plans, and capital allocation decisions.

Part of the challenge is that adaptation feels less glamorous than mitigation. Setting a net-zero target generates positive headlines. Investing in flood barriers and supply chain redundancy doesn't. But for many companies, adaptation investments will deliver more immediate risk reduction than mitigation investments — and the two are complementary, not competing.

Council Fire helps organizations move from climate risk awareness to adaptation action. We bring expertise in physical climate risk assessment, scenario analysis, adaptation strategy development, and integration with broader sustainability and enterprise risk management frameworks. We've found that the most effective adaptation plans are those developed in close collaboration with operations, facilities, procurement, and finance teams — not siloed within sustainability departments.

## The Imperative

Climate adaptation isn't optional, and it's not something you can defer indefinitely. Every year of delay is a year of unmanaged risk exposure. The companies, communities, and countries that invest in adaptation now will be more resilient, more competitive, and better positioned to thrive in a changing climate. Those that don't will learn the hard way that the costs of inaction far exceed the costs of preparation.
