## What is Radiative Forcing?

Radiative forcing (RF) quantifies the change in net energy flux at the top of the atmosphere caused by an external driver of climate change, measured in watts per square meter (W/m²). A positive RF value means the Earth absorbs more energy than it radiates back to space, causing warming; a negative value causes cooling. The IPCC's Sixth Assessment Report estimates total anthropogenic radiative forcing at +2.72 W/m² (±0.96) relative to pre-industrial levels, with CO₂ contributing +2.16 W/m², methane +0.54 W/m², and aerosols partially offsetting warming at roughly −1.1 W/m².

## Why It Matters

Radiative forcing is the fundamental physical mechanism linking human activities to global temperature change. Every greenhouse gas inventory, every climate model projection, and every global warming scenario ultimately traces back to radiative forcing as the causal driver. Understanding RF isn't an academic exercise—it determines the carbon budget remaining before breaching temperature thresholds, informs the pace of required emissions reductions, and explains why different greenhouse gases warrant different levels of policy urgency.

The concept directly informs corporate climate strategy because it reveals which emissions sources have the greatest warming impact per unit. CO₂ dominates total forcing due to sheer volume and atmospheric persistence, but methane's forcing per molecule is approximately 80 times stronger than CO₂'s over 20 years. This distinction matters when companies prioritize abatement investments: a methane leak repair may deliver dramatically more forcing reduction per dollar than an equivalent CO₂ reduction project.

For climate scenario analysis—now required under ISSB, CSRD, and other disclosure frameworks—radiative forcing defines the scenarios themselves. The Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs) used in IPCC projections are named by their end-of-century forcing levels: SSP1-2.6 reaches 2.6 W/m² (consistent with ~1.8°C warming), while SSP5-8.5 reaches 8.5 W/m² (~4.4°C warming). When a company conducts scenario analysis under a "2°C scenario," it's implicitly analyzing a world constrained to approximately 2.6–3.0 W/m² of total forcing.

Emerging research on non-CO₂ forcing agents adds complexity. Aviation's contrails and contrail-induced cirrus clouds contribute an estimated +0.06 W/m²—roughly 57% of aviation's total climate impact—yet receive no attention in standard emissions reporting. Black carbon (soot) from incomplete combustion is the second-largest warming agent after CO₂ in some analyses. These non-GHG forcing agents are increasingly relevant to comprehensive climate impact assessment.

## How It Works / Key Components

Radiative forcing operates through the greenhouse effect and albedo modification. Greenhouse gases absorb outgoing longwave (infrared) radiation emitted by Earth's surface and re-radiate it in all directions, including back toward the surface. This traps additional energy in the climate system. Each gas absorbs at specific wavelengths—CO₂ at 15 micrometers, methane at 3.3 and 7.7 micrometers—and the degree of forcing depends on both the gas's absorption strength and the current saturation of those wavelength bands.

The logarithmic relationship between CO₂ concentration and forcing is a key feature: each doubling of CO₂ produces approximately the same amount of additional forcing (~3.7 W/m²). This means the first 280 ppm of CO₂ (pre-industrial levels) produced much more forcing per ppm than the next 140 ppm. Current concentrations above 420 ppm continue adding forcing, but with diminishing returns per additional molecule. However, because emissions continue accumulating, total forcing keeps rising.

Effective radiative forcing (ERF) is a refined metric that accounts for rapid atmospheric adjustments (within days to weeks) before surface temperatures respond. ERF is now the preferred metric in IPCC AR6 because it better predicts the eventual temperature response. The difference matters for aerosols and some gases where atmospheric adjustments significantly modify the initial forcing.

Feedback mechanisms amplify or dampen the temperature response to forcing. Water vapor feedback (warming evaporates more water, which is itself a greenhouse gas) roughly doubles the warming from CO₂ alone. Ice-albedo feedback (warming melts reflective ice, exposing darker surfaces that absorb more heat) adds further amplification. The equilibrium climate sensitivity—how much warming results from a doubling of CO₂—integrates all feedbacks and is estimated at 2.5–4.0°C by the IPCC, with a best estimate of 3°C.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How does radiative forcing differ from global warming potential?

Radiative forcing measures the instantaneous change in Earth's energy balance caused by a change in atmospheric composition, expressed in W/m². GWP integrates that forcing over time (typically 100 years) relative to CO₂, producing a dimensionless index used for emissions accounting. RF is the physical measurement; GWP is the policy metric derived from it. A gas can have high forcing per molecule but low GWP if it breaks down quickly, or vice versa. Scientists use RF for climate modeling; corporations and regulators use GWP for reporting and compliance.

### Why do aerosols partially offset greenhouse gas warming?

Sulfate aerosols and other reflective particles emitted primarily from fossil fuel combustion and volcanic eruptions scatter incoming solar radiation back to space and increase cloud reflectivity, producing negative radiative forcing of approximately −1.1 W/m². This "global dimming" effect has masked roughly a third of greenhouse gas warming. As air pollution regulations reduce sulfate emissions—particularly from shipping (the IMO 2020 fuel sulfur cap) and coal power plants—this masking effect diminishes, potentially accelerating near-term warming. Some researchers attribute part of the anomalous 2023 ocean warming to reduced shipping aerosols.

### How is radiative forcing used in corporate climate scenario analysis?

The SSP/RCP scenarios used in IPCC-aligned climate risk assessment are defined by their end-of-century radiative forcing levels. When a company runs physical risk analysis under a "high emissions" scenario (SSP5-8.5, 8.5 W/m²) versus a "Paris-aligned" scenario (SSP1-2.6, 2.6 W/m²), the radiative forcing pathway determines the projected temperature, precipitation, sea level, and extreme event changes at each location. Understanding which forcing pathway the world is tracking—currently roughly consistent with SSP2-4.5—helps companies calibrate which scenarios to weight most heavily in strategic planning and capital allocation decisions.
